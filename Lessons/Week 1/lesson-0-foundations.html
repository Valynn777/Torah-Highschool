Part 1 — Foundation & Background
📖

Learn

Foundation

The TorahTorah (תּוֹרָה)HebrewInstruction or teaching — broader than "law." Yehovah's guidance for living before Him. is the first five books of Scripture: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. The word Torah means instruction or teaching — not merely legal code. These books lay the foundation for everything that follows. They tell us about creation, covenant, redemption, holiness, and the ways of Yehovah. Every other section of Scripture builds on what the Torah establishes.

The TanakhTanakh (תנ״ך)HebrewAn acronym from Torah (תּ), Nevi'im (נ), and Ketuvim (כ) — the three major sections of the Hebrew Scriptures. is the full Hebrew Scripture, often called the Old Testament. The name is an acronym built from three Hebrew words: Torah (Instruction), Nevi'im (Prophets), and Ketuvim (Writings). So when we say Tanakh, we mean the Scriptures from Genesis through Chronicles — the entire Hebrew canon.

The books often called the New Testament come after the Tanakh. In this curriculum we call them the Apostolic Writings. They do not stand outside the earlier Scriptures or replace them. They fit after the Torah, Prophets, and Writings, and they continue the same story — showing Messiah, the proclamation of the kingdom, the witness of the apostles, and instruction for the assemblies. To understand the Apostolic Writings well, you must know the Tanakh they stand upon.

🏛️
History Connection

The Tanakh was not written all at once. It was composed across roughly a thousand years — from Moses through Ezra and Nehemiah. The Torah was given first, then the Prophets wrote during the periods of the kings and exile, and the Writings were collected last. Understanding this long historical span helps you see that the same God was speaking faithfully to His people across many centuries and circumstances.

We study Scripture because Yehovah tells us that his word is good, true, and profitable. We also study because we love him. Love is not empty feeling — if we love him, we want to know him, understand his ways, learn what pleases him, and walk in the instruction he has given. We are not gathering information simply to store it. We are studying so that we may know his word, treasure it, and live faithfully before him.

🔎
Logic & Rhetoric

Notice the structure of 2 Timothy 3:16 — it is a single claim with four supporting purposes: teaching, reproof, correction, training in righteousness. This is a common rhetorical form called a list of uses. When you read a claim followed by a list, ask: do the uses listed actually follow from the claim? In this case, what would it mean for Scripture to be "breathed out by Elohim" — and how does that foundation make each of the four purposes make sense?

Going Deeper: Why "Apostolic Writings" and Not "New Testament"?

The phrase "New Testament" is itself a translation of a Latin phrase — Novum Testamentum — which comes from the Latin Bible. The word testamentum (testament) translates the Greek diatheke, which means covenant. So "New Testament" originally meant "New Covenant" — a phrase drawn from Jeremiah 31:31.

Calling these books the "Apostolic Writings" keeps the focus on what they actually are: the witness and letters of the apostles and their close associates. It also helps avoid the impression that the earlier Scriptures are old in the sense of outdated or set aside. The Torah and Prophets remain living and authoritative — the Apostolic Writings build on them, not over them.

Going Deeper: What Are the Nevi'im?

The Nevi'im (Prophets) is itself divided into two parts in the Hebrew tradition. The Former Prophets includes Joshua, Judges, Samuel, and Kings — the historical narrative of Israel in the land. The Latter Prophets includes Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the twelve shorter prophetic books (sometimes called the Minor Prophets in English Bibles).

This matters because the prophets were not only predicting the future — they were calling Yehovah's people back to the Torah in their present moment. Understanding the prophets well requires knowing the Torah first. When Isaiah or Amos speaks of covenant faithfulness, they are standing on what was established in the five books of Moses.

Going Deeper: What Is the Ketuvim?

The Ketuvim (Writings) is a rich and varied collection. It includes poetry and wisdom literature (Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Song of Songs, Ecclesiastes), short narratives and stories (Ruth, Esther), a prophetic vision (Daniel), laments (Lamentations), and historical books (Ezra, Nehemiah, Chronicles). Together these books show how Yehovah's people wrestled with praise, suffering, wisdom, exile, and restoration — all within the framework of the covenant.

Part 5 — History & Context
🌿

History & Scripture

Context

Knowing what Scripture is matters — but it is also important to understand why we study it and how to approach it well. The Tanakh was written across more than a thousand years, preserved through exile, copied by hand for centuries, and translated into Greek (the Septuagint), Latin (the Vulgate), and eventually into English. Each of these moments in history is worth knowing.

📜
History — Transmission of Scripture

The Hebrew Scriptures were preserved and copied by scribes called Soferim, and later by a group called the Masoretes (7th–10th centuries AD), who developed a detailed system of marks and counts to ensure the text was copied without error. The care they gave to transmission is one reason we can have confidence in the text we read today. This history of careful preservation is itself part of understanding what Scripture is.

🗣️
Language — Hebrew, Greek, Latin

The Torah and Tanakh were written primarily in Hebrew (with small portions in Aramaic). The Apostolic Writings were written in Greek. The Latin Vulgate (translated mainly by Jerome in the late 4th century) became the standard Bible of the Western church for over a thousand years. English Bibles stand at the end of a long chain of translation — which is one reason learning even basic Hebrew and Latin words enriches how you read.
