Background & Themes

Before you begin: Read Exodus 1–5 in full in your Torah or Bible before working through this lesson. Everything below is meant to help you reflect on what you have already read, not to replace reading.

Exodus opens with a nation — not just a family. The sons of Israel who came to Egypt with Jacob have multiplied greatly, just as Elohim promised Abraham. But what began as a place of rescue under Joseph has become a house of slavery. Pharaoh, a king who knows nothing of Joseph or his Elohim, sees the growing people of Israel as a threat. The opening chapters of Exodus set the stage for one of the most defining events in all of Scripture: the redemption of Elohim's people out of bondage.

Exodus 1 describes the rapid multiplication of Israel in Egypt and Pharaoh's growing fear. He enslaves the people and commands the death of Hebrew male infants. The midwives — Shiphrah and PuahShiphrah & PuahHebrewTwo Hebrew midwives who feared Elohim more than Pharaoh and refused his command to kill newborn boys. — fear Elohim more than Pharaoh and refuse. Their courage is honored. Even as oppression increases, Israel continues to grow.

Exodus 2 introduces Moses. Born to a Levite family, hidden for three months, and then placed in a basket on the river by his mother, he is found and raised by Pharaoh's own daughter. Moses grows up in the palace but sees the suffering of his people. When he kills an Egyptian who was beating a Hebrew, he flees to Midian, marries, and lives as a shepherd in a foreign land. Meanwhile, back in Egypt, the people cry out under their bondage — and Elohim hears.

🏛️
History Connection — Egypt and Slavery

Large-scale forced labor was a known feature of ancient Egyptian society, especially for major building projects. Records from ancient Egypt show Semitic people working under Egyptian overseers. The pattern described in Exodus — forced labor on cities, brick-making, oppressive taskmasters — fits precisely the kind of labor system attested in the ancient Near East. This was not a comfortable life. The suffering described is real and the cry of Israel rises from genuine affliction.

Exodus 3 is the turning point: the burning bush. Yehovah appears to Moses in the wilderness of Midian. The bush burns but is not consumed. Elohim identifies Himself as the Elohim of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — the covenant Elohim of the patriarchs. He tells Moses He has seen the suffering of His people, heard their cry, and is sending Moses to Pharaoh to bring them out. Moses pushes back with five objections, and Yehovah answers each one. He reveals His name — I AM WHO I AMEhyeh Asher EhyehHebrew"I AM WHO I AM" — related to the covenant name Yehovah. It speaks of His self-existence, faithfulness, and presence with His people. — and gives Moses signs to confirm his calling.

✍️
Logic & Rhetoric — Moses's Objections

In Exodus 3–4, Moses raises five separate objections to his calling. List them as you read. Then notice how Yehovah responds to each one — not with frustration, but with provision. This pattern (objection → assurance → sign) is worth examining closely. Moses is not punished for his questions. He is answered. What does this tell you about how Yehovah deals with those He calls?

Exodus 4 continues the encounter. Moses's final objection — that he is not a good speaker — is met with both a rebuke and a provision: Aaron, his brother, will speak for him. Yehovah also gives Moses three signs: a staff that becomes a serpent, a hand made leprous and restored, and water turned to blood. Moses returns to Egypt. The elders of Israel hear his words and believe. They bow down and worship when they hear that Yehovah has seen their suffering.

Exodus 5 brings the first confrontation with Pharaoh. Moses and Aaron say: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast to me in the wilderness." Pharaoh refuses. He increases the burden on Israel — now they must gather their own straw while still meeting the same quota of bricks. The people turn against Moses and Aaron. Moses cries out to Yehovah in anguish. These chapters do not end in easy victory. The story is honest: obedience does not always bring immediate relief. But Yehovah's purpose is moving forward.

🔬
Science & the Natural World — The Burning Bush

The burning bush that is not consumed stands apart from ordinary fire. Fire is a rapid oxidation reaction that consumes its fuel. A bush that burns without being consumed is not explained by natural chemistry — it is a sign, something outside the normal order. Scripture does not try to explain it away. The point of the sign is precisely that it is not ordinary. Elohim is getting Moses's attention. How does the miraculous function alongside the natural world in these chapters?

Preservation Through Oppression

The more Pharaoh oppresses Israel, the more they multiply. His plans to destroy them fail. Elohim's purposes cannot be stopped by human power.

The Covenant Remembered

Elohim identifies Himself as the Elohim of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. He has not forgotten His promises. The covenant made centuries earlier now moves toward fulfillment.

Elohim Sees and Hears

Exodus 2:24–25 says Elohim heard their groaning and remembered His covenant. He is not distant or unaware. He sees suffering and responds.

Called and Equipped

Moses does not see himself as qualified. Elohim does not call him because he is capable — He equips what He calls. The signs, the words, and the brother are all provision.

Fear of Elohim vs. Fear of Man

The midwives feared Elohim rather than Pharaoh. This is presented as wisdom and faithfulness, not rebellion. Fearing Elohim gives one the courage to do right even under pressure.

Obedience Without Immediate Results

Moses obeys, yet Pharaoh hardens. Israel suffers more. Exodus 5 shows that faithfulness does not always produce instant change. Yehovah's purposes work on a longer timeline.

Commentary: Why Joseph? — The Bridge from Genesis to Exodus

Exodus 1:8 says "there arose a new king over Egypt, who did not know Joseph." This is a hinge verse. The entire protection that Israel had in Egypt rested on one man's favor — and that man was now forgotten. This is a reminder that human protection is always temporary. Rulers change, memories fade, political winds shift. What does not change is Elohim's covenant with Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — which is exactly how Elohim identifies Himself when He speaks to Moses in chapter 3.

The preservation of Joseph's family in Genesis becomes the setup for the redemption of a nation in Exodus. Elohim was working in Joseph's suffering toward a purpose larger than Joseph's lifetime could contain.

Commentary: The Name — I AM WHO I AM (Exodus 3:14)

When Moses asks Elohim His name, the answer given is one of the most profound statements in all of Scripture: Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh — "I AM WHO I AM." This name speaks of absolute self-existence. Yehovah does not derive His being from anything else. He simply is. He was, He is, and He will be — the One who defines and sustains all existence.

This name is also deeply personal and covenantal. It is given in the context of a promise: "I will be with you." The great "I AM" is not a distant philosophical concept but the One who goes with His people into Egypt and out of it again. Every time the name Yehovah appears in Scripture, it carries the weight of this moment at the bush.

Going Deeper: Passover in These Chapters — What Is Being Set Up?

Exodus 1–5 does not yet describe the Passover in full — that comes in Exodus 12. But these chapters are building toward it. The blood of Hebrew infants in chapter 1, the basket of Moses saved from the water in chapter 2, the sign of blood in chapter 4 (water turned to blood), and the first demand to "let my people go, that they may hold a feast to me" in chapter 5 — all of these set the stage. Pharaoh's repeated refusal will bring the ten plagues, culminating in the night of Passover when the destroyer passes over the blood-marked houses of Israel. Knowing where the story is going changes how you read what comes before it.

History & Context

Context

Exodus 1–5 picks up the story of Israel hundreds of years after Joseph's death. These chapters span a long stretch of time — from the multiplication of Israel under a friendly Pharaoh, to enslavement under a hostile one, to Elohim's call of Moses at the burning bush, to the first confrontation with Pharaoh.

Exodus 1
Israel multiplies in Egypt. A new Pharaoh enslaves them. The midwives fear Elohim and resist his command to kill Hebrew infants.
Exodus 2
Moses is born, hidden, found, raised in Pharaoh's house. He flees to Midian after killing an Egyptian. Israel's cry rises to Elohim — and He hears.
Exodus 3–4
The burning bush. Yehovah reveals His name and commissions Moses. Moses returns to Egypt. The elders of Israel hear and believe.
Exodus 5
First confrontation with Pharaoh. He refuses, increases the burden. The people despair. Moses cries out to Yehovah.
Exodus 6 onward
Yehovah renews His promise. The plagues begin. These events come after the scope of this lesson.
🏛️
Ancient Egypt — Power and Religion

In ancient Egypt, Pharaoh was considered divine — a god on earth, the son of Ra. His word was absolute law. When Moses stands before Pharaoh and says "Yehovah says, let my people go," he is not simply delivering a political message. He is declaring that the Elohim of a nation of slaves outranks the living god of the most powerful empire on earth. The ten plagues that follow are often understood as direct confrontations with specific Egyptian deities — Elohim demonstrating His superiority over every power Egypt worshipped.

🔬
Science — Signs and the Natural Order

In Exodus 4, Yehovah gives Moses three signs: a staff turned to a serpent, a hand made leprous and restored, and water turned to blood. Each sign is a reversal or transformation of natural processes — a staff is dead wood, not a living animal; leprosy does not simply vanish; water does not turn to blood. These are not tricks or illusions. They are demonstrations of authority over the created order. The One who made nature is showing that He can alter it. As you read further into Exodus, consider: what is each sign designed to prove, and to whom?
